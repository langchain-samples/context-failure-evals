## Mixed Employee Reviews

**Platform Engineer | Current Employee | New York, NY**
⭐⭐⭐☆☆ **3/5** | *Posted 3 weeks ago*

*Work-Life Balance: 3/5 | Compensation: 4/5 | Culture: 3/5 | Management: 3/5*

Materialize is a company in transition, and that brings both opportunities and challenges. The technical problems are genuinely interesting - we're building database infrastructure that nobody has figured out before. Our small team of about 25 engineers punches way above its weight, and the code quality is generally high. Nate Stewart has been doing a better job as CEO lately, though the transition from the previous leadership was bumpy. Compensation is competitive ($175K + equity) and the benefits are solid. The Austin office has good energy when I visit, and the remote-first culture mostly works well.

However, the rapid growth has created some issues. We've gone from startup chaos to trying to implement more process, but we're somewhere awkward in the middle. Some teams are very well-managed, others feel rudderless. The product roadmap changes frequently, which can be frustrating when you're deep into implementing something that gets deprioritized. Work-life balance is generally good, but there are periods of crunch when customer issues arise. The engineering culture is collaborative, but there's tension between the "move fast" mentality and the need for reliability as we grow our customer base. Overall it's a decent place to work if you can handle some uncertainty and want to work on cutting-edge problems.

**Customer Success Manager | Current Employee | Remote (Colorado)**
⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ **4/5** | *Posted 1 month ago*

*Work-Life Balance: 4/5 | Compensation: 3/5 | Culture: 4/5 | Management: 4/5*

Really enjoying the role overall, though there are definitely growing pains. When I started 10 months ago, we were a tight-knit team of maybe 40 people total. Now we're pushing 200+ and trying to figure out how to scale our processes. The product is genuinely valuable to customers once they understand it, but the market education piece is challenging. Frank Morrison is very accessible and knows the customer base well, which is refreshing coming from a larger company where leadership felt distant.

The customer success team has great autonomy and the customers we work with are doing genuinely innovative things with streaming data. Compensation is fair for a startup ($110K + equity) though probably below what I could get at a more established company. The benefits are improving as we grow - they just added better parental leave and increased the 401k match. The remote culture is strong, though I sometimes feel disconnected from the London team due to timezone issues. The main challenge is that customer expectations are rising faster than our feature development, so there's constant pressure to manage expectations while delivering value. But that's a good problem to have - means we have product-market fit. The career growth potential is real if you can handle the ambiguity of a scaling startup.

**Engineering Manager | Current Employee | Austin, TX**
⭐⭐⭐☆☆ **3/5** | *Posted 6 weeks ago*

*Work-Life Balance: 2/5 | Compensation: 4/5 | Culture: 3/5 | Management: 3/5*

The good: Working on genuinely hard technical problems with a team of very smart people. The database technology we're building is legitimately innovative and customers are excited about it. Compensation is strong ($210K + significant equity) and they've been transparent about the fundraising process. Nate Stewart has a clear vision for where the company is heading and communicates it well in all-hands meetings. The Austin office culture is collaborative and the engineering practices are generally solid.

The challenging: Managing a team through hypergrowth is exhausting. We've scaled from 12 engineers to 80+ in 18 months, and hiring has been the bottleneck for everything. I spend more time on recruiting and onboarding than actual engineering management. The product requirements change frequently as we learn more about customer needs, which makes sprint planning difficult. Work-life balance has suffered as we try to keep up with customer demands and competitive pressure. Some processes that worked well for our smaller team don't scale, but we haven't had time to properly re-architect them. The London team operates somewhat independently, which creates coordination challenges. Overall it's rewarding work but definitely not for everyone - you need to be comfortable with constant change and ambiguity. The equity upside is potentially significant if we can execute on the vision.

**Technical Writer | Former Employee | Remote**
⭐⭐⭐☆☆ **3/5** | *Posted 2 months ago*

*Work-Life Balance: 4/5 | Compensation: 2/5 | Culture: 3/5 | Management: 2/5*

Mixed experience overall. The product is technically fascinating and the engineering team really knows their stuff. Documentation for a streaming database is genuinely challenging work - you're explaining complex distributed systems concepts to developers who may not have that background. The team was small when I was there (maybe 35 people total) which meant lots of direct access to engineers and product managers. Frank Morrison was approachable and understood the importance of good documentation for developer adoption.

However, the role was poorly defined and compensation was below market ($85K for senior technical writing). The company was trying to figure out whether technical writing belonged in marketing, product, or engineering, and I got shuffled between teams twice. The remote culture was generally good, though the London team had different expectations about response times and documentation standards. Left for a role with better compensation and clearer career progression, but I'd consider coming back if they figure out how to properly support technical writing as a function. The technology is genuinely exciting and if they achieve their vision, the documentation challenge will only get more interesting. Just needs better organizational support and competitive compensation for non-engineering roles.

**VP of Marketing | Current Employee | New York, NY**
⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ **4/5** | *Posted 1 week ago*

*Work-Life Balance: 3/5 | Compensation: 5/5 | Culture: 4/5 | Management: 4/5*

Joined 8 months ago to help scale the marketing function and it's been a rewarding challenge. The market opportunity is real - streaming data is becoming critical for more companies, and our approach is genuinely differentiated. We've grown from maybe 60 people to over 180 since I started, and the leadership team has done a good job maintaining culture through that growth. Nate Stewart gives marketing a real seat at the table and understands the long-term nature of developer marketing.

The product-market fit is strong but the market education piece is significant. We're often the first streaming SQL solution that prospects have evaluated, so sales cycles are long but conversion rates are high. The team is collaborative and the cross-functional work with engineering and sales is smooth. Compensation is very competitive (significantly above my previous role) and the equity feels meaningful given the growth trajectory. The Austin and NYC teams work well together, though we're still figuring out the right balance of distributed vs centralized marketing functions.

The main challenges are typical of a scaling startup - limited resources, competing priorities, and the need to build scalable processes while still moving quickly. Work-life balance can be challenging during campaign launches or big events, but management is generally respectful of boundaries. The London office adds complexity to campaigns and messaging, but also gives us better coverage for European prospects. Overall, it's an exciting time to be building the marketing function at a company with this much potential.
