# Materialize Closes Massive $185M Series C to Revolutionize Real-Time Data Infrastructure

**The streaming database startup aims to capture the exploding real-time analytics market with Kleiner Perkins leading the round**

Materialize, the New York-based streaming database company, today announced it has closed a $185 million Series C funding round led by Kleiner Perkins, with participation from existing investors Lightspeed Venture Partners and Redpoint Ventures. The round brings the company's total funding to over $280 million since its founding in 2017, positioning it as one of the most well-capitalized players in the rapidly growing real-time data infrastructure space.

The company, which has grown to over 320 employees (some estimates put the figure closer to 350) across offices in New York, San Francisco, and London, plans to use the funding to accelerate product development and expand its go-to-market efforts as enterprises increasingly demand real-time insights from their streaming data.

"We're seeing an inflection point where every company needs to be a real-time company," said Materialize CEO and co-founder Nate Stewart in an exclusive interview. "The old paradigm of batch processing and stale dashboards is becoming a competitive disadvantage. Our customers are building applications that require fresh, consistent views of their data as it changes."

The funding comes at a time of explosive growth for the real-time data market. According to recent analyst reports, the streaming analytics market is expected to reach $50 billion by 2027, growing at a compound annual growth rate of 28%. This growth is driven by the proliferation of IoT devices, the need for real-time fraud detection, and the rise of event-driven architectures in modern applications.

Materialize's core innovation lies in its ability to maintain incrementally updated materialized views over streaming data, allowing developers to write standard SQL queries while getting real-time results. This approach differentiates it from traditional stream processing systems like Apache Kafka Streams or Apache Flink, which require specialized programming paradigms.

"What Nate and the team have built is truly remarkable," said Monica Schnitger, partner at Kleiner Perkins who led the investment. "They've essentially solved the problem of making streaming data accessible to every developer, not just the 1% who can master complex stream processing frameworks. This democratization of real-time data is going to unlock entirely new categories of applications."

The company's customer base has grown 400% year-over-year, with notable enterprises including several Fortune 500 financial services companies, e-commerce giants, and gaming companies. While Stewart declined to share specific ARR figures, industry sources suggest the company is approaching $50 million in annual recurring revenue.

"We're processing billions of events per day for customers who are building everything from real-time recommendation engines to fraud detection systems," explained Materialize CTO Frank McSherry, who previously worked on differential dataflow at Microsoft Research. "The key insight is that most real-time use cases don't actually need complex event processing – they need fresh, consistent views of their data that they can query with SQL."

The competitive landscape in streaming databases has intensified significantly over the past 18 months. Traditional players like Confluent have expanded beyond messaging into stream processing, while newer entrants like RisingWave and Decodable are pursuing similar approaches to Materialize. Cloud providers Amazon, Google, and Microsoft have also launched competing services, though industry experts suggest these lack the sophisticated incremental computation capabilities that Materialize offers.

"The market is definitely heating up, but we believe our approach to incremental view maintenance gives us a significant technical moat," Stewart noted. "While others are focused on throughput and scale, we're focused on making streaming data truly usable for application developers. It's a fundamentally different philosophy."

The Series C funding will enable Materialize to expand its engineering team significantly, with plans to double its workforce over the next 18 months. The company is particularly focused on hiring senior engineers with expertise in distributed systems, query optimization, and developer experience.

Beyond hiring, the company plans major investments in its cloud platform, which currently runs on AWS but will expand to Google Cloud and Microsoft Azure by early 2024. The multi-cloud strategy is driven by customer demand, particularly from large enterprises with existing cloud commitments.

"Enterprise customers are asking for deployment flexibility," said Sarah Chen, Materialize's VP of Product, who joined from Snowflake earlier this year. "They want the option to run Materialize in their preferred cloud environment, and some even want hybrid deployments for data residency requirements."

The timing of the funding round is notable given the broader pullback in venture capital, particularly for later-stage companies. However, investors appear bullish on infrastructure companies with clear product-market fit and growing revenue streams.

"Infrastructure is one of the few categories where we're still seeing robust investor interest," noted market analyst Jennifer Walsh from Bessemer Venture Partners, who wasn't involved in the round. "Companies like Materialize that are solving real technical problems with measurable ROI are continuing to attract capital, even in this environment."

Looking ahead, Materialize faces several key challenges as it scales. The company must continue to differentiate itself in an increasingly crowded market while also expanding beyond its initial developer audience to reach business users and data analysts. The company has hinted at plans for a more visual query interface and pre-built connectors for popular business intelligence tools.

The real-time data infrastructure market has seen significant consolidation over the past year, with acquisitions including Imply's purchase of real-time OLAP startup Druid, and speculation continues around potential exits for other players in the space. However, Stewart emphasized that Materialize remains focused on building an independent company.

"We're in this for the long haul," Stewart concluded. "The real-time data market is still in its early innings, and we believe the companies that will win are those that can make streaming data as easy to work with as batch data. That's exactly what we're building."
